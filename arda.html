A Brief History of Arda

From the Music of Creation to the End of the Third Age

Ainulindalë

The Music of Creation

In the beginning Eru Ilúvatar created spirits named the Ainur and taught them how to make music. Ilúvatar commanded that they make a great music based on a theme of his own design. The first to be named and most powerful Ainu, Melkor (later called Morgoth by the elves) disrupted the theme, integrating his own more chaotic melodies and symphonies. A grand battle of melodies takes place, each side going back and forth, the music beside Ilúvatar being deep and wide and beautiful but slow and blended with an immeasurable sorrow, from which its beauty chiefly came, while the harmonies of Melkor were loud, vain, and endlessly repeated. Despite Melkor's best efforts to overthrow the Great Music, his most powerful and triumphant notes were taken by the other and woven into its own solemn pattern. Ilúvatar created new themes that enhanced these melodies past the understanding of the Ainur.

The Three Themes

The Great Music of the Ainur progresses thus: Ilúvatar introduces a First Theme to the choir of the Ainur and Melkor corrupts it, converting many of the Maiar to his cause in later years. Next, Ilúvatar imposes a Second Theme, and again Melkor corrupts it. Ilúvatar then proposes a Third Theme, which is also infected by Melkor and devolves into a raging disharmonious conflict. Ilúvatar puts an end to the disharmony with one chord, deeper than the Abyss, higher than the Firmament. This music laid the seeds of much of the history of the yet unmade universe and the people who were destined to dwell therein.

Valian Years

The Vision Made Real

Once the great melody ends, Ilúvatar stops the music and reveals its true meaning to the Ainur through a vision. This vision showed the Ainur what the transliteration of their collaborative Great Music into a material reality would be like. It revealed that the music had a purpose far beyond simply its composition: it was a highly detailed template of the entire history, beginning to end, of a material universe that could exist within time. Moved by this vision of the birth, life and death of the universe, some of the most powerful and influential Ainur felt compelled to experience its events directly. Ilúvatar therefore decided to create Eä, the universe itself. Some of the Ainur decided to go down into the universe to share in its formation. But upon arriving in Eä, the Ainur found it formless and shapeless, for time was just now beginning. The Ainur took it upon themselves to shape the universe and fill it with many things far beyond the grasp of Men. In time, the Ainur formed Arda, the abiding place of the children of Ilúvatar, Elves and Men. The first fifteen of the Ainur who descended to Arda, and the most powerful ones, were called Valar, and the Ainur of lesser might who followed were called Maiar. The Valar settled in Arda to watch over it and help prepare it for the awakening of the Children.

Days before Days

The First War

Immediately after the creation of Arda, the first power struggle between the Valar and Melkor began. Arda was crafted by the labors of all the Ainur combined, but Melkor came to Arda claiming it all for his own. This resulted in the Valar rising against Melkor. The conflict raged for many years; Melkor prevailed for a long time in Arda, destroying and undoing everything the Valar had carefully crafted. When the Ainu Tulkas learnt about this conflict he descended into Arda, and hearing the laughter of Tulkas, Melkor fled beyond the Walls of Night. This power struggle became known as the First War. The war ended but Arda lay marred and the world lay unfinished. Tulkas and Aulë labored for many years to restore it.

The Spring of Arda

With many years of hard labor, the Valar healed and restored Arda. At this point Arda was flat with one ring-like continent covering most of its surface, encircled by Ekkaia, the Encircling Sea. In the center of this continent there stood a great lake; within that lake the Island of Almaren could be found. Two large lamps, Iluin and Ormal, were created to illuminate the lands. The Vala Aulë forged two great towers, Helcar in the furthest north and Ringil in the deepest south, upon which the two great lamps were placed. The Valar lived between the two towers on the Island of Almaren. The period when the Lamps shone was known as the Spring of Arda; during this period the first animals and plants came to be.

The Destruction of the Lamps

Whilst Tulkas was resting after a long period of hard work, Melkor saw an opportunity. He returned to Arda and raised the Iron Mountains in the north, where he built and erected the stronghold of Utumno. Eventually he came down from the Iron Mountains and destroyed the Two Lamps. Melkor also broke apart the lands and caused new seas to form. The Island of Almaren was destroyed in this process. The Spring of Arda came to its end, and all living things began to wither and die. To preserve some of the life in Arda, Yavanna put all remaining living things into a great slumber. The Valar retreated to the western lands of Aman, where they began growing the Two Trees and erected the Pelóri mountain range.

Years of the Trees

Telperion and Laurelin

After the destruction of the Two Great Lamps, Yavanna sang the Two Trees of Valinor, Telperion the Silver Tree and Laurelin the Gold Tree, into existence. The trees were grown on the hills of Ezellohar, watered by Nienna's tears, and illuminated Aman. Yet their light did not reach Middle-earth, thus plunging it into years of pure darkness. The number of years remained the same but were now measured and accounted for as the Years of the Trees, starting with the first blooming of Telperion.

The First Age

Awakening of the Elves

The Elves were awakened in Cuiviénen on the eastern shores of the Sea of Helcar, and thus began the First Age. This part of Arda was still bereft of light, as the lands east of the Pelóri mountain range were not graced by the light of the Two Trees. All living beings still lay in their deep slumber, save for the Elves, the Dwarves woke soon thereafter. They were soon approached by both the Valar and by Melkor. Many of the Elves were persuaded to journey westward toward Aman, but not all of them. The Valar declared war upon Melkor as he started to corrupt the newly awakened Elves. The war raged for nine long years, but in the ninth year the Valar laid siege upon the stronghold of Utumno and captured Melkor, bringing him to Valinor. Melkor was cast into the Halls of Mandos for three hundred years; Sauron, Melkor's lieutenant, evaded capture.

The Destruction of the Two Trees

After three hundred years Melkor was released from the Halls of Mandos. Fueled by his envy for the Elves, because of whom he was captured, he sought to cause discord between the newly established Elven kingdoms. Melkor killed the Elven king of the Ñoldor, Finwë, and stole Fëanor's Silmarils. With the help of Ungoliant he destroyed the Two Trees, snuffing out the only sources of light in Arda. Thus was put a swift end to the Years of the Trees.

The Second Age & the Years of the Sun

The Rise and Fall of Númenor

Númenor prospered for three thousand years. Initially the Kings of Númenor were friends with the Elves, but as their kingdom grew more prosperous the Númenóreans grew prouder and distanced themselves from the ways of their fathers. A disguised Sauron was captured by the last King of Númenor but quickly rose through deceptive means to becoming the King's advisor, and was the primary reason for the downfall of Númenor.

The Realms in Exile

Following the downfall of Númenor, Elendil, a descendant of a Númenórean royal house, came to Middle-earth and established his realms in exile alongside his sons Anárion and Isildur. Sauron also returned to Middle-earth; this time the Elves and the realms of Men formed an alliance resulting in Sauron's defeat and in the deaths of Elendil and Gil-galad. Later the realms in exile split into two independent kingdoms upon Isildur's death, the line of Isildur inherited Arnor while the line of Anárion inherited Gondor. The One Ring was lost; both realms prospered for a millennium, following which Sauron began to stir evil once more. By the end of the second millennia the line of kings had failed and fallen.

The Third Age

The Years of the Sun

The Years of the Sun began when the Valar sent two vessels into the sky, one carrying the fiery fruit from Laurelin, which became the Sun, and the other carrying a shining silver flower from Telperion, which became the Moon. Men awoke at the rise of the first Sun, eventually establishing kingdoms and enjoying long years of peace. But this peace ended abruptly when the conflict between Melkor, now known as Morgoth, and the Elves intensified, with numerous battles known as the Battles of Beleriand taking place. The kingdoms of Men fell to the power of Morgoth. Finally, at the request of Eärendil, the Host of the West came to Middle-earth and vanquished Morgoth. He was exiled from Eä and sent into the endless void. Most of the Elves were summoned back to Valinor, though a few chose to stay in Middle-earth. The Valar granted the Isle of Númenor to the descendants of the Men who had assisted them during the war.

The War of the Ring

The One Ring was rediscovered and Sauron returned once again. The task of destroying the One Ring was given to a humble hobbit, Frodo Baggins, and with him went the Fellowship of the Ring. The One Ring was destroyed at the fires of Mount Doom, and Sauron was vanquished once and for all. The time of the Elves ended in Middle-earth, Aragorn was crowned King of the Reunited Kingdom, and thus concluded the Third Age.

"The world is indeed full of peril, and in it there are many dark places; but still there is much that is fair, and though in all lands love is now mingled with grief, it grows perhaps the greater." - J.R.R. Tolkien, The Fellowship of the Ring
