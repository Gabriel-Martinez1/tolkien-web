Peoples of Arda

A Record of the Free, the Fallen, and the Ancient

Higher Beings

Ainur

Also known as: The Holy Ones, the Great Music-makers

Overview

The Ainur are the first and greatest beings created by Eru Ilúvatar, the One, the supreme deity of Tolkien's cosmology. They are angelic spirits of immense power and wisdom, existing before the physical world was made, and it was through their voices that the world came into being.

Origins

Before the creation of the world, Ilúvatar made the Ainur from his own thought and taught them music. He then proposed a great theme, and the Ainur sang together in what became the Ainulindalë,the Music of the Ainur, a vast and intricate symphony through which the history of the world was laid down in sound before it was ever given physical form. When Ilúvatar showed them a vision of what their music had made, and then spoke the word that gave it existence, the world,Arda, came into being. Those Ainur who chose to descend into the physical world to shape and govern it became bound to it, their power and identity intertwined with its fate. These are known as the Valar and the Maiar.

Nature

The Ainur are pure spirits, they have no inherent physical form, though they are capable of taking one. Their true nature is more akin to thought, purpose, or musical theme than to any bodily creature. The greatest among them, the Valar, took physical forms that reflected their nature and domains,Ulmo manifesting as a great lord of the sea, Yavanna as a figure of green and gold associated with growth, and so on. Lesser Ainur, the Maiar, similarly take forms suited to their purpose or, in the case of the fallen, to their corruption.

The Valar

The Valar are the fourteen greatest Ainur who entered the world. They dwell in Valinor, the Undying Lands beyond the western sea, and serve as the powers and governors of the world under Ilúvatar. Among the most significant are:

  • Manwë - King of the Valar, lord of air and wind, the closest to Ilúvatar in thought
  • Varda (Elbereth) - Queen of the Valar, maker of the stars, beloved above all by the Elves
  • Ulmo - lord of all waters, who never left the seas and worked quietly to aid the peoples of Middle-earth throughout its history
  • Yavanna - lady of all growing things, maker of the Two Trees of Valinor, who petitioned for the creation of the Ents
  • Aulë - the smith, maker of the physical substance of the world, who in his impatience created the Dwarves before Men were made
  • Mandos (Námo) - keeper of the halls of the dead, who pronounces the dooms of Arda
  • Melkor (Morgoth) - the greatest of the Ainur in power, who fell into darkness through pride and the desire to create independently of Ilúvatar. He became the first Dark Lord, the source of all evil in Middle-earth, and the ultimate enemy against whom all the wars of the First Age were fought.

Fate

The Valar remain in Valinor beyond the reach of the mortal world. At the end of time, the Dagor Dagorath, the final battle, the Ainur are said to take part in the remaking of the world.

Maiar

Also known as: Istari (when sent to Middle-earth as Wizards), lesser Ainur

Overview

The Maiar are Ainur of lesser power than the Valar, spirits who served and assisted the great powers in the shaping and governance of the world. They are beings of immense capability by mortal standards, and yet they are subordinate to the Valar in the hierarchy of divine beings. Some remained faithful and serve as emissaries and guardians; others fell into darkness and became some of the most terrible servants of evil the world has known.

Nature and Appearance

In their natural state the Maiar are spirits without fixed form, as all Ainur are. When taking physical form they typically appear as Men, but with greater physical and mental power, deeper wisdom, and a presence that sets them apart from ordinary mortals. Those sent to Middle-earth as the Istari (Wizards) took the bodies of old Men deliberately, limiting their power so as to guide and inspire rather than dominate.

The Istari - Wizards of Middle-earth

In the Third Age, the Valar sent five Maiar to Middle-earth in the form of Wizards to assist the Free Peoples against the rising shadow of Sauron. They were forbidden from matching Sauron's power directly or seeking to dominate Men - their purpose was to counsel, encourage, and inspire. The most important of these are:

  • Saruman the White (Curunír) - the eldest and initially the greatest of the Wizards, head of the order and the most skilled in the arts of craft and machinery. His deep study of Sauron's methods led to his corruption, he desired the One Ring for himself and sought to become a power in his own right. He was expelled from the order by Gandalf after his treachery was exposed, and was ultimately slain by his own servant Gríma Wormtongue.
  • Gandalf the Grey (Mithrandir, Olórin) - the most beloved and ultimately the most effective of the Wizards. Humble, wise, and deeply compassionate, he was the one who most faithfully fulfilled the purpose for which the Istari were sent. After his battle with the Balrog of Moria he was returned to life as Gandalf the White, elevated to head of the order in Saruman's place. He was a servant of the secret fire and wielder of Narya, the Ring of Fire, one of the three Elven Rings.
  • Radagast the Brown (Aiwendil) - a lover of birds and beasts, deeply attuned to the natural world. He was less concerned with the affairs of Men and Elves than with the creatures of forest and field, and largely faded from the major events of the age. Though sometimes considered to have strayed from his purpose, he was not corrupt, simply more interested in the living world than in politics or war.
  • The Blue Wizards (Alatar and Pallando, or Morinehtar and Rómestámo) - two Wizards who traveled into the far east of Middle-earth and largely disappeared from the records of the west. Their ultimate fate and purpose are among the great unanswered questions of the legendarium. Tolkien suggested in later writings that they may have founded secret cults or movements in the east, and that their work may have contributed to weakening Sauron's hold over the eastern peoples.

Notable Non-Istari Maiar

  • Sauron - originally a Maia of Aulë, corrupted by Morgoth in the earliest ages and becoming his chief servant. After Morgoth's defeat he became the second Dark Lord, the primary antagonist of the Second and Third Ages. He forged the One Ring to dominate the other Rings of Power and thus control the free peoples of Middle-earth.
  • Melian - a Maia of great power and wisdom who came to Middle-earth and dwelt among the Elves of Beleriand. She became the queen of Doriath, wife of the Elven king Thingol, and the mother of Lúthien, the greatest Elf-maiden in history. She wove the Girdle of Melian around Doriath, an enchantment that no power could pass without her will.
  • Ossë and Uinen - Maiar of the sea, servants of Ulmo. Ossë is wild and stormy; Uinen is gentle and calming. Sailors invoke Uinen for safe passage.
  • Eönwë - the herald of Manwë, among the mightiest of the Maiar, who led the host of the Valar in the War of Wrath at the end of the First Age.

Fate

The faithful Maiar continue to serve the Valar in Valinor. The Istari, having completed their purpose with the defeat of Sauron, departed or faded from the world. Gandalf sailed to the Undying Lands at the end of the Third Age alongside Frodo, Bilbo, and the remaining Elven Ring-bearers.

Balrogs

Also known as: Valaraukar (Sindarin: "Demons of Might"), Maiar of Morgoth

Overview

Balrogs are Maiar who were corrupted by Morgoth before the creation of the world, drawn into his rebellion during the very first discord of the Music of the Ainur. They are among the most ancient and most terrible servants of the first Dark Lord: enormous beings of fire and shadow, wielding weapons of flame and darkness, and possessing a physical and spiritual power that few beings in Middle-earth could withstand.

Origins

Like all Maiar, Balrogs were originally pure spirits in the service of the Valar. Those who would become Balrogs were seduced by Morgoth in the time before the world was fully shaped, turning from their original purposes and dedicating themselves to his will. They served him throughout the First Age as his most powerful direct servants, second only to Sauron in the hierarchy of Morgoth's forces.

Appearance

When manifested in physical form, Balrogs appear as enormous humanoid figures wreathed in fire and shadow, darkness and flame intertwined, with a presence that radiates terror. They wield whips of flame and carry fiery swords or axes. The full extent of their size is debated — in Tolkien's texts they are described in ways that suggest both man-like scale and something far more enormous — but all accounts agree on the overwhelming dread they inspire.

Power and Abilities

Balrogs are immensely powerful physical and spiritual beings. They can engage in direct combat with Elven lords, Wizards, and other powerful beings, and the fires they command are no mere illusion, they are real and devastating. They are not invincible, however; several were slain during the First Age by great Elven warriors and heroes. Notable among these are:

  • Gothmog, the Lord of Balrogs, the greatest of their number, who slew both Fëanor and Fingon, two of the mightiest Elven kings, before being slain himself by Ecthelion of the Fountain at the Fall of Gondolin
  • The Balrog slain by Glorfindel, at the Fall of Gondolin, the Elf-lord Glorfindel fought a Balrog on a mountain peak; both fell to their deaths, but Glorfindel's sacrifice allowed others to escape

Durin's Bane, The Balrog of Moria

By the Third Age, the great wars of the First Age had destroyed almost all of the Balrogs. The last known survivor was the Balrog dwelling in the depths of Khazad-dûm (Moria), later known as Durin's Bane. It had fled underground after the War of Wrath and lay dormant for thousands of years in the deepest roots of the Misty Mountains. When the Dwarves of Moria, mining for mithril, delved too deep, they broke into its chamber and awakened it. It drove the Dwarves from Moria and killed their king, Durin VI, giving it the name by which it became known.

During the War of the Ring, it encountered the Fellowship of the Ring in Moria, where Gandalf stood against it on the Bridge of Khazad-dûm. After a fall into the deep water far below, then a battle through the underground passages and finally up through the peak of Zirakzigil, Gandalf slew it — at the cost of his own life, which the Valar then restored, returning him as Gandalf the White.

Fate

All known Balrogs are destroyed by the end of the Third Age. Whether any others survived hidden in the deepest places of the earth is not recorded.

Free Peoples

Elves

Also known as: Quendi ("those who speak with voices"), Eldar (those who answered the summons of the Valar), Firstborn, the Elder Kindred

Overview

Elves are the firstborn children of Ilúvatar, the first to awaken in Middle-earth, the most ancient of the speaking races, and by most measures the most gifted. They are immortal, beautiful, and possessed of extraordinary skill in all arts. Their long history is one of immeasurable achievement, terrible tragedy, and gradual departure from the world.

Origins

The Elves first awoke at Cuiviénen, a lake in the far east of Middle-earth, in the Ages of Stars, before the Sun and Moon existed, by the light of stars alone. They were discovered there by Morgoth, who captured some of them and began twisting them, producing the first Orcs. The Valar, learning of the Elves, went to war against Morgoth to protect them and then summoned the Elves to come and dwell with them in Valinor. Those who answered this summons became known as the Eldar. Those who refused, or who turned back during the great journey west, remained in Middle-earth and became the Avari, the Unwilling.

Appearance

Elves are tall and slender, with fair skin, bright and penetrating eyes, typically grey, and fine features. They are uniformly described as beautiful by the standards of Men. Most Elves wear their hair long. They move with a grace and lightness that sets them apart from all other races, leaving no footprints in snow. Their voices are particularly remarkable, clear, melodious, and capable of great power in song.

Immortality and Death

Elves do not age or die of disease. They can be slain in battle or by great grief, but their spirits do not leave the world, they go to the Halls of Mandos in Valinor, where they wait and may eventually be reembodied. This immortality binds them to the world in a way that Men are not bound as the ages pass and the world changes, Elves grow weary in a way that is deeply different from mortal weariness.

Culture and Skills

Elves are gifted in virtually every art: music, poetry, language, smithcraft, architecture, healing, and warfare. They are the inventors of writing in Middle-earth and the creators of some of its greatest works, including the Silmarils (forged by Fëanor), the Rings of Power (forged by Celebrimbor), and the great cities of Gondolin, Nargothrond, and Rivendell. In matters of gender and social role, Elves practice a general equality, though tradition tends toward men specializing in warfare and women in healing, rooted in the belief that taking a life and saving a life are spiritually incompatible arts. Their primary languages are Quenya, the ancient high tongue, and Sindarin, the more commonly spoken tongue in Middle-earth.

Varieties and Kindreds

  • Vanyar - the fairest and most beloved of the Elves by the Valar. Golden-haired, deeply devoted to Manwë and Varda. They remained in Valinor and play little role in the history of Middle-earth.
  • Noldor - the most gifted in craft and knowledge, greatest of the Elven smiths. The Noldor forged the Silmarils, built the great underground cities of Beleriand, and waged the wars of the First Age against Morgoth.
  • Sindar (Grey Elves) - the largest group remaining in Middle-earth, ruled by Thingol in Doriath. The majority of Elves encountered during the Second and Third Ages belong to this broad tradition.
  • Silvan Elves (Wood-elves) - the most numerous Elves of Middle-earth by the Third Age, dwelling in Mirkwood under Thranduil and in Lothlórien under Celeborn and Galadriel. More rustic than the Noldor but deeply in tune with the living forest.
  • Falathrim - the coastal Elves of Beleriand, mariners and craftsmen, who built the great havens of Brithombar and Eglarest.
  • Avari - those who refused the summons to Valinor and remained in the east. Little is known of them.

Places of Inhabitation

  • Rivendell (Imladris) - the refuge of Elrond Half-elven, one of the last great Elven strongholds in the Third Age and a haven of lore and healing
  • Lothlórien - the golden wood ruled by Galadriel and Celeborn, protected by the power of Nenya, the Ring of Water
  • Mirkwood (Eryn Lasgalen) - the great forest of the north, home to the Silvan Elves under King Thranduil and his son Legolas
  • The Grey Havens (Mithlond) - the ancient port on the Gulf of Lune, ruled by Círdan the Shipwright, from which the Elves sail to the Undying Lands
  • Gondolin, Nargothrond, Doriath - the great hidden kingdoms of the First Age, all destroyed by the end of that age

Notable Figures

  • Fëanor - greatest of the Noldor smiths, creator of the Silmarils and the Elvish letters. His pride and the oath he swore set in motion much of the tragedy of the First Age.
  • Lúthien Tinúviel - daughter of Thingol and the Maia Melian, considered the most beautiful being ever to live in Middle-earth. She and the mortal man Beren stole a Silmaril from Morgoth's crown.
  • Galadriel - the greatest of the Noldor remaining in Middle-earth in the Third Age, bearer of Nenya, Ring of Water.
  • Elrond Half-elven - lord of Rivendell, bearer of Vilya the Ring of Air, counselor and healer across multiple Ages.
  • Legolas - prince of Mirkwood, member of the Fellowship of the Ring, whose friendship with Gimli became one of the most celebrated bonds of the age.

Fate

As the Third Age drew to a close the Elves began their final departure from Middle-earth, sailing from the Grey Havens to the Undying Lands. The destruction of the One Ring also unmade the power of the Three Elven Rings, causing much of what they had sustained, Lothlórien and Rivendell, to fade. The Fourth Age belongs to Men, and the Elves pass out of the world's keeping.

Dwarves

Also known as: Khazâd (in their own tongue, Khuzdul), Naugrim (Sindarin: "Stunted People"), Gonnhirrim ("Masters of Stone")

Overview

Dwarves are a proud, enduring, and deeply private people — masters of stone, metal, and craft, whose works rival and in some respects surpass those of any other race. They are stubborn, long-memoried, fierce in battle, and fiercely loyal to their own kind. Their history is one of great achievement and great tragedy in equal measure.

Origins

The Dwarves were not originally part of Ilúvatar's design. The Vala Aulë, the great smith, desired so keenly to have beings to teach his craft to that he created the Seven Fathers of the Dwarves in secret, before the Elves had awoken. When Ilúvatar revealed that he knew of this act, Aulë humbly offered to destroy what he had made. Moved by Aulë's sincerity, Ilúvatar instead chose to adopt the Dwarves as his own children, granting them true life, with the stipulation that they would not awaken until after the Elves. Their dead go to the Halls of Aulë, and there is a Dwarvish belief that at the end of the world they will help Aulë rebuild it.

Appearance

Dwarves are short and stocky, taller than Hobbits but broader and more powerfully built, with great physical endurance and strength. They are known for their thick beards, braided or forked, which are a point of great personal and cultural pride. Both male and female Dwarves grow beards, which has historically caused confusion among outsiders, a source of some amusement to the Dwarves themselves.

Culture and Society

Dwarves are organized into clans descended from the Seven Fathers, each associated with a particular stronghold or mountain range. Their economy is almost entirely mineral-based, mining precious metals and gems, working stone, and trading with Men and Elves. They are a deeply secretive people: their true language, Khuzdul, is never taught to outsiders, and their true names are kept private. Dwarves are long-lived, most living up to 250 years. They are resistant to the corrupting influence of the Rings of Power, though the Seven Dwarf Rings inflamed their natural greed and ultimately led most bearers to ruin.

Varieties and Clans

  • Longbeards (Durin's Folk) - the most prominent clan, associated with Khazad-dûm, Erebor, and the Iron Hills. Thorin Oakenshield and his company belong to this clan.
  • Firebeards and Broadbeams - two clans originally associated with the Blue Mountains of Eriador
  • Ironfists and Stiffbeards - eastern clans, little documented in western records
  • Blacklocks and Stonefoots - the most distant eastern clans, almost entirely absent from western histories

Places of Inhabitation

  • Khazad-dûm (Moria) - the greatest Dwarf kingdom ever built, beneath the Misty Mountains, famous for its mithril seams, abandoned after awakening Durin's Bane
  • Erebor (the Lonely Mountain) - sacked by the dragon Smaug and reclaimed during the events of The Hobbit; one of the wealthiest Dwarf-holds in the Third Age
  • Iron Hills - a stronghold east of Erebor, home to a significant population of Durin's Folk
  • Blue Mountains (Ered Luin) - home to the Firebeard and Broadbeam clans, and later a refuge for homeless Dwarves
  • Aglarond (the Glittering Caves) - the vast cave system beneath Helm's Deep; after the War of the Ring, Gimli led a colony here
  • Grey Mountains (Ered Mithrin) - once a major Dwarf settlement, largely abandoned after attacks by Cold-drakes

Notable Figures

  • Durin the Deathless - the eldest and most revered of the Dwarf-lords, founder of Khazad-dûm, said by some to be repeatedly reincarnated in their greatest kings
  • Thorin Oakenshield - King of Durin's Folk in exile, leader of the quest to reclaim Erebor from Smaug; slain at the Battle of Five Armies
  • Gimli son of Glóin - member of the Fellowship of the Ring, who forged an extraordinary friendship with the Elf Legolas; later granted passage to the Undying Lands, the only Dwarf ever to receive such an honor
  • Dáin Ironfoot - King under the Mountain after Thorin's death, who fell defending Erebor during the War of the Ring

Fate

The Dwarves continued into the Fourth Age, with Erebor and Aglarond as their primary strongholds. Their long-term fate is one of slow diminishment, their numbers never fully recover from repeated catastrophes, and the wealth of their ancient kingdoms is largely spent.

Free Men

Also known as: Edain (the faithful Men of the First Age), Dúnedain (the descendants of Númenor)

Overview

Men are the second-born children of Ilúvatar, awakening in the east of Middle-earth at the first rising of the Sun. Unlike Elves, Men are mortal, they age, die, and their spirits pass beyond the circles of the world entirely, to a fate unknown even to the Valar. This has led to Men being called the Twice-given, for their mortality, though often seen as a curse, is by some accounts understood as a gift, a freedom from the circles of the world that the immortal Elves do not share.

Origins

The earliest Men to enter the west of Middle-earth and ally themselves with the Elves against Morgoth became known as the Edain. As a reward for their faithfulness, the Valar raised a great island kingdom out of the sea for them: Númenor. The Númenóreans became the greatest civilization of Men in the history of Middle-earth, but pride and the fear of death eventually led them to turn against the Valar. Ilúvatar intervened directly, drowning Númenor beneath the sea. Only the faithful escaped on ships, led by Elendil and his sons Isildur and Anárion. These survivors became the Dúnedain, and they founded the kingdoms of Gondor in the south and Arnor in the north.

Notable Varieties

  • Dúnedain - descendants of the faithful Númenóreans; taller, longer-lived, and wiser than most Men. By the Third Age their numbers have dwindled, with the northern line surviving only as the Rangers of the North.
  • Rohirrim - the Men of Rohan, descended from the Northmen of the Vales of Anduin. A proud people of horsemen and warriors, fiercely loyal to their allies.
  • Men of Bree - a stout and independent people of mixed descent, living at the crossing of the ancient East-West Road and the Greenway. One of the few communities where Men and Hobbits live side by side.
  • Lake-men of Esgaroth - merchants and traders living in the lake-town built on stilts above the Long Lake near the Lonely Mountain.
  • Men of Dale - a people of the north who once flourished under the shadow of the Lonely Mountain. Rebuilt in the Third Age, Dale became a prosperous kingdom once more.
  • Beornings - descendants of the skin-changer Beorn, dwelling in the Vales of Anduin. Fierce fighters with an ancient gift for taking the form of bears.
  • Men of Forochel - a hardy and isolated people living in the frozen northern reaches, deeply suspicious of outsiders and descended from the ancient Forodwaith.

Places of Inhabitation

  • Gondor - the greatest surviving kingdom of the Dúnedain, with its capital at Minas Tirith
  • Rohan - the kingdom of the Rohirrim, a vast plain between the Misty Mountains and the White Mountains
  • Arnor - the northern Dúnedain kingdom, long since broken and eventually lost entirely
  • Bree - a small crossroads village in Eriador
  • Esgaroth - the lake-town on the Long Lake, a center of trade in the northeast
  • Dale - a prosperous city-kingdom at the foot of the Lonely Mountain
  • Vales of Anduin - home to the Beornings and scattered communities of Northmen
  • Forochel - the frozen far north, home to the remnant Forodwaith people

Fate

With the defeat of Sauron and the crowning of Aragorn II Elessar as King of the Reunited Kingdom at the dawn of the Fourth Age, the realm of Men entered a new age of dominance. The Elves departed for the Undying Lands, the Dwarves diminished, and Middle-earth passed increasingly into the keeping of Men.

Drúedain

Also known as: Woses, Wild Men of the Woods, Púkel-men, Drûg (singular)

Overview

The Drúedain are among the oldest and most enigmatic varieties of Men in Middle-earth, a remnant people of ancient origin, few in number, deeply private, and almost entirely overlooked by the wider world. Though small and easily dismissed, they are possessed of unique gifts, fierce courage, and an ancient hatred of Orcs that has never diminished.

Appearance

The Drúedain are short and stocky, smaller than Dwarves but taller than Hobbits, with heavy brows, wide flat faces, dark eyes, and broad noses. They grow little body hair beyond that on their heads. Their movements can be slow and deliberate, but they are capable of extraordinary stillness, remaining motionless for hours or even days without moving or blinking, a trait that unnerves those unfamiliar with them. Their stone carvings, known as Púkel-men, are made in their own likeness and are found along ancient roads and hilltops throughout regions they once inhabited.

Abilities and Culture

The Drúedain possess a unique craft sometimes likened to voodoo or sympathetic magic. Their most notable application is the creation of watch-stones, carved figures imbued with a portion of their maker's spirit, which can serve as silent sentinels, warn of danger, or even drain the life of an enemy whose likeness is captured in the carving. They are masters of woodcraft and stealth, deeply attuned to the natural world, and possess an acute awareness of the presence and movements of Orcs, whom they despise above all other beings. Despite preferring to live quietly in deep woods, they extend fierce and selfless protection to neighboring human communities without seeking recognition or reward.

Places of Inhabitation

  • Drúadan Forest - the primary home of the Drúedain in the Third Age, a dense woodland on the north side of the White Mountains near Minas Tirith
  • Eilenach and the beacon hills - Púkel-men carvings along the road through the White Mountains suggest the Drúedain once ranged more widely across this region
  • In earlier ages they were more widespread, inhabiting parts of Beleriand and later migrating westward with other Men

Role in the War of the Ring

During the War of the Ring the Drúedain of Drúadan Forest, led by their chieftain Ghân-buri-Ghân, aided the Riders of Rohan by guiding them through the forgotten Stonewain Valley, a secret path through the White Mountains that allowed them to bypass Orc forces blocking the road to Minas Tirith. In gratitude, King Éomer granted the Drúadan Forest to the Drúedain in perpetuity, forbidding all others from entering without their leave.

Fate

The Drúedain survived into the Fourth Age, their forest granted to them as a free land. Beyond this their fate is not recorded.

Ents

Also known as: Onodrim (Sindarin), Shepherds of the Trees, Tree-herds

Overview

Ents are among the most ancient races of Middle-earth, towering, tree-like beings who serve as the guardians and shepherds of the great forests. They are slow, patient, and deeply deliberate, but when roused to action they are among the most terrifying forces in the world.

Origins

According to legend, Ents were brought into being at the request of the Vala Yavanna, who feared that the trees and growing things of Middle-earth would be cut and burned without anyone to speak for them. The Ents were created, or awakened, to be the voices and defenders of the forests. Over long ages they grew to resemble the trees they tended, becoming as much tree as creature.

Appearance

Ents vary considerably in appearance depending on the type of trees they are most associated with. They are enormous, taller than most Men, with rough bark-like skin, long limbs, and deep root-like feet. Their faces are ancient and lined, and their eyes carry a great depth of age and thought. They move slowly by default but can move with surprising speed and force when urgency demands.

Varieties

  • Birch-ents - slender and pale, associated with silver birch forests
  • Oak-ents - the largest and most powerful variety, immovably strong
  • Rowan-ents - associated with mountain rowans, known to inhabit higher elevations
  • Willow-ents - associated with water-side willows; Old Man Willow of the Old Forest is believed by some to be a willow-ent gone dark and malevolent
  • Treebeard (Fangorn) - the oldest living Ent, a great many-branched figure who serves as the informal elder of the remaining Ents

The Entwives

The greatest sorrow of the Ents is the loss of the Entwives. In ages past the Ents loved wild and growing things, while the Entwives turned to tending ordered gardens, orchards, and cultivated lands. They drifted apart, and eventually the Entwives wandered east and established their own gardens. When the Ents came to seek them again, the Entwives were gone, possibly destroyed during Sauron's wars in the Second Age. Without the Entwives, there are no Entings, and the race slowly dwindles toward extinction.

Places of Inhabitation

  • Fangorn Forest - the ancient forest at the feet of the Misty Mountains, the last true stronghold of the Ents in the Third Age. It is old, dark, and deeply unwelcoming to outsiders.
  • In earlier ages Ents ranged far more widely, tending forests across Middle-earth that have since been felled or diminished.

Role in the War of the Ring

The destruction of trees around Isengard by Saruman's industry, cutting and burning to fuel his furnaces, was the provocation that finally roused the Ents to war. After an Entmoot lasting several days, Treebeard led the Ents in a march on Isengard. They tore down its walls, smashed its machinery, and flooded the entire valley by breaking the dams of the River Isen, leaving Saruman trapped in the tower of Orthanc.

Fate

With the war ended, Fangorn remained the home of the surviving Ents. Their long-term fate is one of slow fading, without the Entwives they cannot continue as a people, and the Fourth Age belongs increasingly to Men. Whether the Entwives were ever found is left as one of Tolkien's deliberately unanswered questions.

Hobbits

Also known as: Halflings, Periannath (Sindarin), Little Folk

Overview

Hobbits are a small, cheerful, and deeply comfort-loving people, remarkable in the wider world primarily for their near-total lack of interest in it. They are farmers, gardeners, and socializers by inclination, and yet on the occasions they have been drawn into the great events of the age, they have proven possessed of a quiet resilience and courage that has astonished far greater and more powerful beings.

Origins

The origins of Hobbits are obscure. They are widely believed to be a branch of the race of Men that diverged long ago, and they share enough in common with Men, mortality, language, general form, to support this theory. Their own records do not reach back far enough to settle the question. They are believed to have originated somewhere in the Vales of Anduin before migrating westward, crossing the Misty Mountains and eventually settling in the region that would become the Shire.

Appearance

Hobbits stand roughly three to four feet tall, half the height of a Man, hence the name Halflings. They have round, well-fed faces, curly or wavy hair, and bright eyes. Their most distinctive feature is their feet, large, tough, and leathery, covered on top with thick curly hair, requiring no shoes in any but the harshest terrain. They tend toward roundness of figure, which they wear as a mark of contentment and good living.

Varieties

  • Harfoots - the most numerous and most typical variety. Smaller and more slender, browner of skin, and the most inclined toward a settled life of farming and comfort. The strain most associated with the classic Hobbit character.
  • Stoors - broader and heavier-built, with larger hands and feet. More comfortable around water than other Hobbits, and the only variety known to grow facial hair. Sméagol (Gollum) was of Stoor descent.
  • Fallohides - the least numerous variety, fairer-skinned and taller. More adventurous and fond of trees and forests. They produced many notable leaders, including the Took and Brandybuck families.

Culture and Way of Life

Hobbits prize comfort, food, and routine above all else. A typical Hobbit eats six meals a day when possible. They live in smials, comfortable, well-furnished burrows dug into hillsides, with round doors and windows. They are fond of pipe-weed, parties, giving and receiving gifts, and genealogy. They maintain meticulous family trees and take great pride in knowing their relations to six or seven degrees. Though largely non-magical and without a warrior tradition, their small size, light feet, and natural stealth make them surprisingly difficult to detect.

Places of Inhabitation

  • The Shire - the heartland of Hobbit civilization, a well-ordered and peaceful land in the northwest of Eriador, divided into four Farthings
  • Buckland - a strip of land on the east bank of the Brandywine River, home to the Brandybuck family and considered slightly adventurous by Shire standards
  • Bree - a small number of Hobbits live among Men in this village, one of the few places where the two peoples coexist
  • Gladden Fields - a marshy region in the Vales of Anduin where a Stoor community once lived, and where Sméagol found the One Ring

Fate

Hobbits played a role in the War of the Ring utterly disproportionate to their size or expectations. Frodo Baggins carried the One Ring to Mount Doom; Samwise Gamgee carried Frodo when he could go no further; Meriadoc Brandybuck struck the crucial blow that felled the Witch-king; and Peregrin Took served in both the army of Rohan and the Guard of the Citadel of Gondor. After the war, the Shire was restored and the Hobbits returned to their comfortable lives, their great deeds largely unrecorded in the wider histories of the age.

Enslaved & Fallen

Fallen Men

Also known as: Easterlings, Southrons, Men of Darkness

Overview

Not all Men stand among the Free Peoples. Those who dwell in the east and south of Middle-earth, long estranged from the legacy of the Edain and the influence of the Valar, fell under the shadow of Morgoth in the First Age and later Sauron in the Second and Third. Whether through willing allegiance, fear, coercion, or centuries of manipulation, many of these peoples became enemies of the Free Peoples and soldiers of the Dark Lord.

Notable Peoples

  • Haradrim - fierce warriors from the southern deserts and jungles of Harad, divided into the Near Harad and Far Harad. The latter produce the great Mûmakil (Oliphaunts), enormous war-beasts armored and fitted with war-towers.
  • Balchoth - a warlike people from the eastern lands of Rhûn who launched a devastating invasion of Gondor's eastern territories in the Third Age, overrunning the province of Calenardhon before being defeated.
  • Wainriders - an eastern people who warred repeatedly against Gondor across much of the Third Age, so named for their great chariots and war-wagons.
  • Men of Khand - a people from the region of Khand, southeast of Mordor, who served among Sauron's forces during the War of the Ring.
  • Corsairs of Umbar - a seafaring people descended partly from renegade Númenóreans, who established the great port-fortress of Umbar. Long enemies of Gondor, they raided its coastlines repeatedly and allied with Sauron during the War of the Ring.
  • Men of Dunland - a rough hill-people bearing ancient grievances against the Rohirrim who displaced them. They allied with Saruman and participated in the assault on Helm's Deep.

Places of Inhabitation

  • Harad - the vast southern lands, from Near Harad bordering Gondor to the deep jungles of Far Harad
  • Rhûn - the broad eastern lands beyond the Sea of Rhûn, home to many Easterling peoples
  • Khand - the southeastern region between Mordor and the eastern deserts
  • Umbar - a great fortified harbor on the southern coast, stronghold of the Corsairs
  • Dunland - the rough hill-country west of the Misty Mountains and south of Rohan

A Note on Culpability

Figures such as Faramir of Gondor reflected openly on the Men of the south and east, noting that they were not born enemies, they were people who had lived long under shadow, deceived and manipulated across generations by Sauron's influence, and that in other circumstances they might have been allies rather than foes.

Ringwraiths

Also known as: Nazgûl, The Nine, Úlairi, Black Riders, the Undying

Overview

The Ringwraiths are the most terrible servants of Sauron and among the most fearsome beings in Middle-earth during the Third Age. They were once great Men, kings, sorcerers, and lords of renown who accepted nine Rings of Power from Sauron and were utterly destroyed by them.

Origins

Sauron forged the Nine Rings during the Second Age and distributed them to powerful Men whose ambition or pride made them vulnerable. The Rings extended their lives far beyond natural span and granted them great power but at a terrible cost. Over time the Rings consumed everything that the wearers were, hollowing them out until they faded entirely from the living world and became wraiths, neither fully alive nor truly dead, bound completely to Sauron's will, incapable of independent desire or action contrary to his purpose.

Appearance

The Ringwraiths have no true physical form remaining. They are perceived by ordinary eyes only through the dark robes and armor they wear to give themselves shape in the living world. Those with the ability to perceive the spirit world see them as they truly are: terrifying, withered, kingly figures of dreadful power. Their presence induces a deep and paralyzing terror known as the Black Breath, and wounds from their Morgul-blades leave splinters that slowly draw victims into the wraith-world.

Notable Members

  • The Witch-king of Angmar - greatest of the Nine, whose true name is not recorded. He founded and ruled the evil realm of Angmar, destroyed the kingdom of Arnor, and later commanded the forces of Mordor. An ancient prophecy held that no living Man could slay him — fulfilled at the Battle of the Pelennor Fields when he was destroyed by Éowyn of Rohan and Meriadoc Brandybuck.
  • Khamûl the Easterling - the second most powerful of the Nine, the only one whose name is recorded. He was a great lord of the Easterlings and served as Sauron's lieutenant in Dol Guldur.
  • The remaining seven are unnamed in surviving records.

Places of Inhabitation

  • Minas Morgul - formerly Minas Ithil, one of the great towers of Gondor, captured and corrupted by the Nazgûl and made their stronghold in the Third Age
  • Dol Guldur - the dark fortress in Mirkwood, where Khamûl served as lieutenant
  • The Nazgûl rode at large across Middle-earth in service of Sauron, with no fixed dwelling beyond Minas Morgul

Fate

With the destruction of the One Ring, the power that sustained the Ringwraiths was broken. All nine were destroyed, their forms consumed and their spirits dispersed. They could not survive the unmaking of the Ring that had unmade them.

Orcs

Also known as: Goblins, Yrch (Sindarin), Uruk (Black Speech), Glamhoth ("din-horde," Sindarin)

Overview

Orcs are among the oldest and most numerous servants of the dark powers of Middle-earth, a race born of corruption and sustained by cruelty, serving as the primary military force of both Morgoth and Sauron across all three Ages.

Origins

The most widely accepted account holds that Orcs were first created by Morgoth during the Ages of Stars, before the first rising of the Sun. He is said to have captured Elves who had wandered from safety and subjected them to torment, sorcery, and slow corruption over generations, twisting them into a ruined mockery race. Tolkien explored alternatives, including the possibility that some Orcs may have been corrupted Men — but the corrupted Elves account remains the most prominent in the legendarium.

Appearance

Orcs are humanoid but grotesque, typically shorter and broader than Men, with coarse skin ranging from grey to black, slanted eyes adapted for darkness, fanged mouths, and bent or gnarled limbs. Their forms are a deliberate ruination, reflecting the corruption at their origin. They are physically tough and capable of great endurance under the direction of a strong will, but without such direction tend toward chaos and infighting.

Varieties

  • Common Orcs - the standard foot soldiers of Morgoth and Sauron's armies. Shorter and weaker than Men, averse to sunlight, quarrelsome and brutal.
  • Snaga - a term meaning "slave" in the Black Speech, applied to smaller, weaker breeds who serve the larger as laborers and messengers.
  • Uruks - larger, stronger Orcs bred by Sauron in Mordor. More disciplined than common Orcs and capable of enduring sunlight.
  • Uruk-hai - Saruman's breed, developed at Isengard. Tall, black-skinned, and powerfully built, capable of running great distances in daylight. Among the most formidable Orc soldiers of the Third Age.
  • Half-orcs - beings of mixed Orc and Man blood, bred by Saruman, who could pass undetected among Men. Used as spies and infiltrators.
  • Great Orcs of Moria - an especially large and powerful variety that inhabited the deeps of Khazad-dûm, larger than typical Orcs and more dangerous in direct combat.

Places of Inhabitation

  • Angband - Morgoth's great underground fortress in the north, the first mass stronghold of Orcs in the First Age
  • Mount Gundabad - a sacred and strategic peak at the northern end of the Misty Mountains, long held as an Orc stronghold
  • Goblin-town - a sprawling underground city beneath the High Pass of the Misty Mountains
  • Moria (Khazad-dûm) - the ancient Dwarf kingdom, occupied by Orcs after its abandonment; home to a large and particularly dangerous population
  • Mordor - the primary domain of Sauron's forces in the Third Age, containing vast Orc armies
  • Isengard - home to Saruman's Uruk-hai in the late Third Age
  • Dol Guldur - the dark fortress in Mirkwood, garrisoned by Orcs under Sauron's lieutenants

Nature and Society

Orcs are fierce, cruel, and deeply quarrelsome. Without a commanding will to direct them they tend toward infighting and dissolution. They have no love for beauty or craft beyond the making of weapons and instruments of war. They speak debased and varying dialects, with the Black Speech of Mordor serving as a common tongue during the height of Sauron's power.

Fate

With the destruction of the One Ring, the Orc armies were broken and scattered. Without the dominant will of Sauron to bind and direct them, they fell into chaos and fled. Whether significant Orc populations survived into the Fourth Age in remote regions is uncertain.

Trolls

Also known as: Torog (Sindarin), Olog (Black Speech)

Overview

Trolls are enormous, brutish creatures of great physical power and limited intellect, created by Morgoth as instruments of war and destruction. They are among the most feared physical opponents on the battlefield, not for cunning or strategy, but for sheer overwhelming force.

Origins

Trolls are believed to have been created by Morgoth in mockery of the Ents, a dark mirror of the great tree-herds, stripped of wisdom, patience, and love of living things and replaced with nothing but destruction. Like most of Morgoth's creations, they are a corruption rather than a true act of making.

Appearance

Trolls are enormous humanoid creatures, standing far above the height of Men, with immensely broad and powerful frames. Their hides are thick and stone-like, and their strength is tremendous. Most are dim-witted, with a limited capacity for language or reason beyond basic cunning. Their expressions are brutal and their temperament violent.

Varieties

  • Stone-trolls - the most commonly encountered type in the northwest of Middle-earth. Slow-witted and crude, capable of some speech in rough dialects. The three trolls encountered by Bilbo and the Dwarves Bert, Tom, and William were of this variety, turned to stone at dawn after being kept arguing through the night by Gandalf's trickery.
  • Hill-trolls - larger and more aggressive, encountered during the War of the Last Alliance at the end of the Second Age.
  • Cave-trolls - dwelling in underground places such as Moria. One attacked the Fellowship in the Chamber of Mazarbul, wounding Frodo before being driven off.
  • Mountain-trolls - great beasts used by Sauron's forces during the siege of Minas Tirith, employed to batter the city's gates and break defensive lines.
  • Snow-trolls - a variety inhabiting the far northern wastes, referenced in older lore but rarely encountered in the Third Age.
  • Olog-hai - the most fearsome variety, bred by Sauron in the late Third Age. Unlike all other Trolls, the Olog-hai could endure sunlight without being turned to stone. They were larger, faster, and more disciplined than any other variety, functioning as focused shock troops under the direct will of Sauron. With the fall of the One Ring, they collapsed into confusion like all of Sauron's servants.

The Curse of Sunlight

The vulnerability of most Trolls to sunlight, which turns them permanently to stone, is one of the most defining and exploited weaknesses in Middle-earth. Its precise origin is unexplained in Tolkien's texts, but it is widely understood as a fundamental flaw in their making: a limitation that Morgoth could not overcome when creating beings designed to operate in darkness. The Olog-hai represent Sauron's partial solution to this problem.

Places of Inhabitation

  • Trollshaws - a wild region in Eriador east of Rivendell, home to roaming Stone-troll populations
  • Misty Mountains - various Troll populations inhabit the mountains and their approaches
  • Moria - Cave-trolls dwell in the deep places
  • Mordor and its surroundings - Mountain-trolls and Olog-hai are concentrated here as part of Sauron's armies
  • The far north - Snow-trolls inhabit the cold wastes beyond the reach of most travelers

Fate

With the fall of Sauron, the Olog-hai lost the animating will that made them effective and fell into disorder. Whether Stone-trolls and other varieties persisted in remote areas into the Fourth Age is not recorded, though scattered populations in places like the Trollshaws and the far north are entirely possible.

Other Beings

Tom Bombadil

Also known as: Iarwain Ben-adar (Sindarin: "Oldest and Fatherless"), Forn (by the Dwarves), Orald (by Men of the North)

Overview

Tom Bombadil is one of the most singular and enigmatic figures in all of Middle-earth. He dwells in the ancient forest between the Shire and the Barrow-downs, alongside his wife Goldberry, the River-daughter. He is jovial, eccentric, and seemingly without ambition, and yet he is regarded by those who know of him as something deeply ancient and wholly unique, a being that fits into no established order or category.

Origins

No one knows who or what Tom Bombadil truly is. This is not merely a gap in the characters' knowledge, it appears to be a deliberate and fundamental mystery that Tolkien himself was content to leave unanswered. He predates nearly everything in Middle-earth. The Elves name him Iarwain Ben-adar, Oldest and Fatherless, suggesting he was present before them, before the Dwarves, and perhaps before much else. Tolkien himself described Bombadil in letters as a deliberate enigma and "an exemplar of pure being" existing simply for the joy of existence, with no desire for power or dominion over anything.

Appearance and Manner

Tom Bombadil is described as a short, stout, and merry figure, old beyond reckoning yet brimming with energy and delight. He wears a tall hat with a blue feather, a coat of bright blue, and yellow boots. He speaks largely in a rolling, rhythmic cadence that borders on song, and he is rarely without some expression of good humor. His wife, Goldberry, is equally radiant and mysterious; the daughter of the River, graceful and serene, her presence filling their home with a sense of timeless peace.

Power and the One Ring

Tom Bombadil's relationship with the One Ring is perhaps the most discussed aspect of his character. When Frodo offers him the Ring, Tom takes it, and does not disappear. When he puts it on himself, it has no effect on him whatsoever. He can even see Frodo clearly when Frodo wears it and becomes invisible to everyone else. The Ring exerts a corrupting influence on virtually every being in Middle-earth who comes near it, yet it appears to hold no power over Tom at all. He seems simply uninterested in it, as though it is no more significant than any other trinket.

When the Council of Elrond discusses the possibility of giving the Ring to Tom for safekeeping, Gandalf cautions against it. Tom's indifference to the Ring, he argues, would ultimately make him an unreliable guardian — he might simply lose it or set it aside without concern for the consequences.

His Domain

Tom's territory encompasses the Old Forest, the banks of the Withywindle, and the Barrow-downs. Within this land he is absolute master. He rescues Frodo and his companions from Old Man Willow, the ancient, malevolent heart of the Old Forest, with a simple song. He later drives away the Barrow-wight that captures the hobbits with equal ease, striding through the dark and cold as though they were nothing at all.

Limitations

For all his power within his own lands, Tom Bombadil's influence does not extend beyond them. He takes no part in the wider struggles of the age, and does not involve himself in the War of the Ring. Whether this is a limitation of his power or simply a reflection of his nature, his complete absence of desire for anything beyond his own world is unclear. Gandalf implies that if Sauron were to conquer all of Middle-earth, Tom would eventually fall too not because Sauron could corrupt him, but simply because everything around him would be consumed.

Significance

Tom Bombadil represents something unusual in Tolkien's legendarium, a being entirely outside the struggle between good and evil, not because he is neutral or indifferent to good, but because the entire framework of power, dominion, and corruption simply does not apply to him. He is one of the few genuinely happy beings in Middle-earth, and perhaps the only one for whom the Ring holds no temptation whatsoever, not through resistance or virtue, but because he simply has no wants that the Ring could offer to fulfill.

"He is his own master. But he cannot alter the Ring itself, nor break its power over others." - Gandalf, at the Council of Elrond

River Spirits

Also known as: Spirits of water, lesser Maiar of Ulmo

Overview

Middle-earth's rivers, lakes, and waterways are not merely geographical features, some are inhabited by or identified with spirits, beings whose nature is tied to the water itself. These are generally understood to be lesser Maiar in the service of Ulmo, the lord of all waters, though their exact nature and degree of consciousness varies.

Notable Figures

  • Goldberry - the most prominent river spirit encountered in the Third Age, known as the River-daughter and associated with the Withywindle. She dwells with Tom Bombadil in the ancient forest and embodies the beauty and peace of clear water and open sky. Her exact nature is never defined, though she is clearly a being of genuine power and otherworldly grace.
  • The River-woman of the Withywindle - Goldberry's mother, referred to only by this name; a deeper and older spirit of the river itself.
  • Various unnamed spirits are implied to inhabit the great rivers of Middle-earth — the Anduin, the Bruinen (whose flood Elrond commanded against the Nazgûl), and others.

Ulmo's Influence

Ulmo, unlike the other Valar, never withdrew entirely from Middle-earth. He moved through all the waters of the world rivers, lakes, rain, and sea and communicated through them with those he wished to aid or warn. His voice could be heard in the sound of water, and those who listened might receive counsel or warning. He worked most actively during the First Age, when he aided Elves and Men repeatedly through the medium of water and dream.

Giants

Also known as: Stone-giants

Overview

Giants are among the most poorly documented beings in Tolkien's legendarium, glimpsed briefly in The Hobbit and referenced nowhere else in detail. Their nature, origin, and place in the order of Middle-earth's peoples is essentially unknown.

What Is Known

During Bilbo and the Dwarves' crossing of the Misty Mountains in a thunderstorm, they witness what appear to be enormous beings hurling boulders at one another across the peaks, though whether these are sentient creatures engaging in sport or conflict, or some other phenomenon, is unclear even to the observers. Tolkien never elaborated on their nature in any of his other writings, and they receive no mention in The Lord of the Rings or The Silmarillion.

Theories

Various interpretations have been proposed that Stone-giants are a variety of Troll, that they are spirits of the mountains similar to how river spirits inhabit water, or that they are simply a folkloric element included in The Hobbit that was not integrated into the deeper mythology. Tolkien himself appears to have left them as a deliberate background mystery, a reminder that Middle-earth contains more beings than any catalogue can contain.

Dragons

Also known as: Worms, Great Worms, Uruloki (fire-drakes), Coldworms (cold-drakes)

Overview

Dragons are among the most fearsome and magnificent creatures in Middle-earth; ancient, immensely powerful, and deeply intelligent. They are creatures of destruction and greed, and their capacity for devastation, physical, psychological, and economic, has shaped the history of entire peoples.

Origins

Dragons were bred by Morgoth in the First Age, first appearing during the wars of Beleriand. Like much of Morgoth's work, they are a corruption or dark mockery of natural things, immense serpentine creatures imbued with fell intelligence, supernatural endurance, and in many cases the power of fire. The first dragon to appear in the histories is Glaurung, the Father of Dragons, whose curse destroyed the lives of Túrin Turambar and Nienor.

Varieties

  • Fire-drakes (Uruloki) - dragons capable of breathing fire. The most destructive and most storied variety. Glaurung was the first, though he lacked wings; later fire-drakes were winged.
  • Cold-drakes - dragons without the fire-breathing ability, inhabiting cold northern regions such as the Grey Mountains. They devastated the Dwarf settlements of the Grey Mountains in the Third Age, driving Durin's Folk south.
  • Winged dragons - the most fearsome variety, combining flight with fire. Ancalagon the Black, bred by Morgoth for the War of Wrath, was the greatest dragon ever to exist. Smaug was a winged fire-drake and the last of the truly great dragons in the Third Age.

Dragon Abilities

  • Fire-breath - the signature weapon of fire-drakes, capable of melting stone and incinerating armies
  • Armored hide - dragon scales are essentially impenetrable except at specific weak points, making direct combat almost impossible
  • Dragon-spell (glámour) - perhaps the most insidious ability. Dragons can exert a powerful hypnotic influence over those who meet their gaze or hear their voice, inducing confusion, paralysis, and false memory. Even Bilbo, conversing with Smaug, is warned by Gandalf never to look into a dragon's eyes.
  • Greed and hoarding - dragons are supernaturally drawn to treasure, which they accumulate and guard with obsessive ferocity. They are also extraordinarily cunning and capable of complex manipulation and riddling.

Notable Dragons

  • Glaurung - the first and Father of Dragons. Wingless fire-drake who led Morgoth's armies and cast the dragon-spell that destroyed Túrin Turambar's family. Slain by Túrin himself.
  • Ancalagon the Black - the greatest dragon ever bred, a winged fire-drake of catastrophic power created for the War of Wrath. Slain by Eärendil with the aid of the Eagles.
  • Scatha the Worm - a Cold-drake of the Grey Mountains, slain by Fram of the Éothéod in the Third Age. His hoard was famously disputed between Men and Dwarves.
  • Smaug - the last of the great dragons, a winged fire-drake who sacked the Dwarf kingdom of Erebor and the town of Dale, and lay upon their treasure for nearly two centuries. Slain by the archer Bard of Esgaroth, who struck the one bare patch on his belly - a weakness revealed to Bilbo in conversation.

Places of Habitation

  • Angband - bred and first deployed here by Morgoth during the First Age
  • Grey Mountains (Ered Mithrin) - the Cold-drakes devastated Dwarf settlements here in the Third Age
  • Withered Heath - a blasted wasteland northeast of the Grey Mountains, believed to be a dragon breeding ground in the Third Age
  • Erebor - occupied by Smaug for approximately 170 years until his death

Fate

By the end of the Third Age, Smaug's death leaves no great dragon still active in the histories of the west. Whether lesser dragons persisted in the far north or east is unknown. Tolkien suggested in some writings that dragons may play a role in the final battle at the end of the world.
